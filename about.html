What is tabletop miniature wargaming?

To be perfectly honest it is mainly a way for grown ups to play with toy soldiers. There is a little bit more to it than that though. Today it is a combination of several different activites. Playing the games is usually a social activity. Building and painting the models and terrain is a creative one, just as coming up with armies and scenarios is. Many historical wargamers engage in a lot of study of history, from as specific as researching uniforms of units to more sweeping understanding of general development of human history.

Social

Most games are social activities. Recreational wargaming often is less about winning and more about playing out the story. What happens on the battlefield ("battlefield" is the pretentious word often used to describe the playing area.) is as entertaining to the one giving the orders as to the one being the victim of them. Many gamers spend more time running groups, clubs and events than actually playing the games.

Creativity

The miniatures, the "toy soldiers" are usually bought, but often modified and nearly always painted before putting them on the table. Building terrain to make the table visually pleasing is a hobby all of its own. As is 3-D modelling and printing, that have become more and more a part of the miniature wargame hobby the last years, with printers becoming more affordable and easier to use. Fantasy gaming often involves writing up background stories for armies, battles, worlds and individuals. It certainly does incentivises people to read.

History

Games like chess, go or hnefatafl have been used to teach military skills for thousands of years. In the 19th century the Preussian army came up with their "Kriegsspiel" (simply "Wargame" in German") with rulesets much less abstract and much more focused on simulating actual military action.

In 1913 author and known pacifist H.G. Wells published a book named "Little Wars". This is widely recognized as the first set of rules for recreational wargaming. While Little Wars incorporated many of the elements we today see in miniature wargaming (as - miniatures, terrain modelled to scale and measuring of distances its rules for combat outcomes were still rudimentary. Up until the 1950’s the hobby still was miniscule. In the UK, Donald Featherstone published a series of books on wargaming. In 1956 Jack Scruby of California organized the first miniature wargaming convention in America, one year after he began producing cheap miniatures intended for wargaming out of type metal. It had 14 attendants, but now the ball was rolling. Another milestone that also happened in the 50s was the first miniature wargame rule set for medieval combat by Tony Bath. This turned out to be a great inspiration to Gary Gygax Chainmail in 1971, which evolved into Dungeons & Dragons 1974. About ten years later the UK company Games Workshop published the first edition of Warhammer The Mass Combat Fantasy Role-Playing Game. In 1969 Wargames Research Group was founded and published the ruleset War Game Rules: 1000 B.C. to 500 A.D. The following years rule sets generally became more and more involved and complex. In 1988 Phil Barker (one of the founders of Wargames Research Group) tried out a experimental set of rules at the Society of Ancients conference. It was a minimalistic ruleset of merely 2 pages, played on a small table only 2’x2’, with only 12 elements of 1-5 miniatures a side. The game caught on, and in 1990 De Bellis Antiquitatis was published. Keeping the philosophy of the experimental rules and adding list for armies covering 2000 years of warfare and most cultures on earth it became immensely popular. Only one year later a fantasy version was published. Hordes of the Things.

Why is it a small game?

Compared to many other miniature games Hordes of the Things can be considered as a very small one for several reasons:

Low Minature Count

Hordes of the Things is a game of battles, rather than a skirmish game, the the difference being that a skirmish game is about individual combatants whereas a battle game is about units. Unlike many battle games however you don't need an enormous amount of miniatures to play. An army typically consists of 10-14 elements of troops, each element consisting of one to five miniatures mounted on a singe base.

Space Requirement.

Depending on the miniature scale chose the game is usually played on a battlefield 600 mm by 600 mm, or 900 mm by 900 mm. A pub table is sufficient, and you might even have space for your beverage of choice.

Time Requirement

Playing a game of Hordes of the Things rarely takes more time than an hour, including setting up everything and putting everything away.

Why is it a big game?

At the same time the game gives the feeling of large battles, sweeping movement of large bodies of troops, and a true ebb and flow of battle. Short turns make for a game where both players are involved all through it. It is quite possible to play the game with more than one army a side for what can be truly epic battles.

A brief description of the game.

  • Aerial Heroes—Heroes who can fly, or have a flying mount
  • Airboats—Large flying platforms, like zeppelins
  • Artillery—Powerful, long ranged missile weapons
  • Beasts—Savage creatures, like wolves
  • Behemoths—Elephants, Giants, Dinosaurs, etc.
  • Blades—Well equipped foot soldiers, like the Roman legion
  • Clerics—Religious leaders who perform an anti-Magician role
  • Dragons—Large, super-intelligent flying creatures
  • Fliers—Giant Eagles or other dangerous flying creatures
  • Gods—Powerful supernatural entities, like Zeus
  • Heroes—Mighty warriors of renown
  • Hordes—Endless seas of poorly trained and equipped fighters
  • Knights—Armored horsemen who charge ferociously into mêlée
  • Lurkers—Creatures that hide in dark places; water lurkers are a specialised version that live in aquatic terrain
  • Magicians—Spell casters, sorcerers and conjurers
  • Paladins—Warriors of Great Virtue
  • Riders—Mounted soldiers who fight with missile weapons in preference to mêlée
  • Shooters—Soldiers who fight with missile weapons, such as archers and crossbow men
  • Sneakers—Ninjas and other sneaky assassin types
  • Spears—Well ordered soldiers fighting in phalanx
  • Warband—Ferocious soldiers who lack discipline but not bravery
